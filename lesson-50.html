Peer Evangelism

Duration: ~60 minutes
Date: November 22, 2026
Age Group: 13–14 years
Objective: To demystify sharing faith with peers — stripping away the pressure to perform or convince — and help teens see evangelism as natural conversation rooted in genuine care and a real story.
1. Opener: What Do You Picture? (5 min)
5 min
First Impressions

Ask: “When you hear the word ‘evangelist’, what image comes to mind?”

Let a few people answer. Likely: someone shouting on a street corner, a pushy salesperson, an awkward conversation. Acknowledge those images honestly — then say:

“Everything about that picture is wrong for what we’re talking about today.”

2. Clearing the Ground: Three Myths (15 min)
15 min
Myth 1
You need to have all the answers
Truth
Nobody has all the answers. The most honest and disarming thing you can say to a sceptical friend is “I don’t know — but that’s something I’ve actually wondered too.” Certainty isn’t the goal. Honesty is.
Myth 2
Evangelism means convincing people
Truth
Your job is not to argue someone into faith. That’s not even possible. Your job is to live a life that raises questions, and to answer those questions honestly when they come. Conviction belongs to the Holy Spirit — not to you.
Myth 3
You need a special moment or a formal presentation
Truth
The most effective evangelism among peers happens in ordinary moments — a comment in passing, how you respond when things go wrong, what you talk about on the bus home. It’s less a event and more a posture.
3. What Actually Works: Your Story (15 min)
15 min
The Power of a Personal Testimony

Nobody can argue with your experience. Theological debates can go in circles, but “this is what happened to me” is impossible to dismiss.

A testimony doesn’t need to be dramatic. It needs to be true. Three simple questions shape it:

Before: What was life like, or what did I believe, before faith became real to me?
Turning point: What happened? What shifted?
Since: What’s different now? What does faith actually do in my everyday life?

Activity: Give each teen 5 minutes to jot down rough answers to these three questions. Then in pairs, share a shortened version (90 seconds max).

4. Natural Entry Points (10 min)
10 min
Opening Lines That Don’t Sound Weird

Faith rarely comes up unless someone opens the door. Here are real, low-pressure ways the conversation can start:

A
When a friend shares a problem
Listen fully first. Then, if the friendship allows it: “Can I be honest? Something that’s helped me with stuff like this is praying about it. I know that sounds strange — do you want me to?”
B
When someone asks why you’re different
This is a gift. Don’t deflect. Be direct: “Honestly, a lot of it comes from my faith. It’s shaped how I think about things. If you’re curious, I’m happy to talk about it.”
C
When spiritual topics come up naturally
In a conversation about death, meaning, fairness, the future — lean in instead of changing the subject. Ask questions. Share what you actually think. Curiosity is contagious.
The goal is not to have a perfect prepared speech.
The goal is to be the kind of person your friends feel safe asking real questions.
5. The Commission & Close (15 min)
15 min
You’re Already Sent
“Therefore go and make disciples of all nations… teaching them to obey everything I have commanded you.”Matthew 28:19–20

The Great Commission isn’t addressed to missionaries or pastors — it’s the last thing Jesus said to a group of young, ordinary people before he ascended. That includes you.

“All nations” sounds enormous. Your version of it right now is your school, your neighbourhood, your group chat. That’s your world, and you’re already in it.

This Week’s Challenge

Name one friend who doesn’t share your faith. This week, your only job is to invest in that friendship — a message, a hangout, a genuine question. Not an agenda. Just presence.

“Let your light shine before others, that they may see your good deeds and glorify your Father in heaven.”Matthew 5:16

Closing Prayer: “Lord, you spent your life with people who didn’t yet believe. Help us do the same — not with pressure or performance, but with genuine love. Give us the words when we need them, and the courage to open our mouths. Amen.”
